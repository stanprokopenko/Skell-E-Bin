Mastering Material Properties in Art

As an art student, it's crucial to be an observer of light. Understanding what is happening with the light and how to replicate these effects on different materials in your artwork is essential. This knowledge allows you to mimic real-world material properties and light interactions accurately.

Basic Reflections and Material Properties

Specular and Metallic Reflections

  • Specular reflections are like chrome reflections, reflecting the environment sharply.
  • Turning on a metallic property in 3D software shows how an object reflects its surroundings.
  • Blurring the reflection slightly and adding a bumpy surface simulates a soft brushed metal look, still reflecting the environment but in a softened manner.

Diffuse Reflections

  • Completely blurring the reflection results in a diffuse reflection, where there are no specular highlights, only bounce light.

Glossy Reflections

  • Adding a gloss layer over a diffuse reflection creates the effect of materials like ceramic or glossy plastic, combining diffused and specular reflections but maintaining the local color in the diffuse reflection.

Advanced Material Properties

Refraction and Its Variations

  • Clear refraction in materials like glass allows light to pass through, bending and refracting parts of the environment.
  • Blurring and diffusing the refraction simulates softer materials like frosted glass or soft plastic.

Local Color and Light Absorption

  • Local color determines how much light is absorbed and reflected. For example, a red object absorbs all colors except red.
  • Changing the material properties while keeping the local color, like making a cyan object metallic, affects the reflections and appearance of the object.

Special Optical Effects

Subsurface Scattering

  • Subsurface scattering is when light penetrates an object, scatters below the surface, and exits, creating a diffused look without seeing through the object.
  • This effect is different from diffused refraction, where you can still see inside the object.

Fresnel Effect

  • The Fresnel effect is based on the angle of light incidence, where reflection increases with the angle.
  • This effect can be used to simulate a soft, fine haze on surfaces, making them appear soft to the touch.

Anisotropic Reflections

  • Anisotropic reflections occur when highlights are stretched due to fine scratches on a surface, changing the material's appearance, like brushed metal.

* * *

Understanding and replicating the interactions between light and material properties in your artwork is fundamental. By observing these effects in the real world and applying them to your 3D renders or paintings, you can create more realistic and visually appealing art.
