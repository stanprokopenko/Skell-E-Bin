Types of Reflections

Copy Code

As an artist, it's crucial to be an observer of light and understand how it interacts with various materials. By studying these interactions, we can learn to mimic them in our artwork. In this article, I'll explore different types of reflections, refractions, and material properties using 3D rendered examples.

Types of Reflections and Materials

3D rendering software allows us to change material properties and see how they affect reflections. Let's look at some examples.

Specular Reflection (Metallic)

  • A specular reflection, like in chrome, reflects the surrounding environment.
  • By turning on the metallic property, the material will reflect the room.
  • Slightly blurring the reflection and adding a bumpy surface creates a brushed metal look.

Diffuse Reflection

  • A diffuse reflection has no specular highlights, only bounce light.
  • Adding a gloss layer over a diffuse reflection can mimic ceramic or glossy plastic.
  • This maintains the local color in the diffuse reflection while adding a specular reflection on top.

Refraction

Refraction occurs when light passes through a material and bends.

  • Clear glass allows light to pass through, showing the room's reflection and light.
  • Blurring the refraction slightly creates a soft plastic or frosted glass appearance.
  • Further diffusion leads to an even softer refraction effect.

Local Color and Material Properties

Local color determines which wavelengths of light are absorbed or reflected by an object.

  • Changing material properties affects how the local color is perceived.
  • A cyan object can be made to look metallic, glossy plastic, or diffuse by adjusting the material settings.

Diffused Refraction vs. Subsurface Scattering

  • Diffused refraction: Light softens as it passes through the material, but the inside is still visible.
  • Subsurface scattering: Light enters the material, scatters inside, and exits without allowing transparency. The light spreads inside and then comes back out.

Fresnel Effect

The Fresnel effect describes how light reflection changes depending on the viewing angle.

  • At flat angles, more of the local color is visible.
  • At 90-degree angles, there is an increased amount of reflection.
  • This effect can be used to create a soft, velvety appearance, like peach fuzz on a surface.
  • Example: Looking at a lake, you can see under the water near your feet, but farther away, you only see the sky's reflection.

Anisotropic Reflections

Anisotropic reflections create the appearance of brushed metal.

  • Highlights are stretched due to fine scratches in the surface.
  • These scratches change the material properties of the surface.

By understanding these different light and material interactions, we can incorporate them into our artwork to create more realistic and visually interesting pieces. Take the time to observe how light behaves on various surfaces and practice mimicking those effects in your chosen medium.
