My FASTEST Technique for Realistic Oil Paintings

Copy Code

When painting roses outdoors, I need to work fast before the light changes. My goal is to achieve a realistic result as quickly as possible. There's no time for any drawing, so I immediately go for the most intense color that I see.

Mixing Colors for the Rose Petals

  • I start with the warm yellow-green in the middle of the rose facing me, made with cadmium yellow, viridian, and titanium white.
  • As I move towards the outsides of the petals, I gradually adjust the color to be cooler and lighter by adding more white, yellow ochre, and viridian.
  • For shadows under light petals, I mix a darker, more intense green using cadmium yellow, viridian, and yellow ochre.
  • To carve out light petals, I create a cooler, grayer shadow color with titanium white, viridian, and raw umber.
  • For subtle variation on lighter petals, I mix a pale gray-violet color using titanium white, ultramarine, alizarin crimson, and a small amount of yellow ochre.

Placing Shadows and Accents

  • I place a few notes of the pale gray-violet color on some of the lighter petals.
  • Using a small soft head brush, I add warm dark accents with pure yellow ochre within shadows under small petals.
  • I use the cool green shadow color to place a few more small shadows under petals.

Simplifying the Subject

  • I focus on the middle of the rose as the main center of interest.
  • Due to time constraints, I choose to include only the most obvious details.
  • By including just a few essential details, I demonstrate how the rose can appear lifelike.
  • Our attention automatically focuses on small areas of detail, but we never see everything in focus at the same time.
  • Slavishly copying every detail from a photograph does not result in a truly lifelike appearance.

Softening Edges and Creating Depth

  • I carve out the outer edges of the petals with a dark green mixture.
  • Using a large fan brush, I mask in the rest of the background, creating a transparent texture similar to foliage.
  • I soften edges between the other two roses and the background using paper towel.
  • With a palette knife, I create a hard edge at the top of the main rose.
  • To soften some petals, I place a middle-value violet along their edges.
  • Only some of the hardest, most prominent edges should be included, while the rest are softened to varying degrees.

Blocking in the Other Roses and Leaves

  • I block in the other two roses facing away from me, mainly in shadow.
  • I accent one or two petals on the top rose but keep edges soft on the bottom rose.
  • I consider the different colors visible in the roses: warm and cool greens, and a gray-violet on the underside of some petals.
  • Using a mixture of viridian, raw umber, and a small amount of alizarin crimson, I place dark shadows amongst the leaves.
  • By indicating just one or two leaf edges, I demonstrate how they appear more lifelike.
  • For color variation, I paint some leaves with a warmer, more intense green made with yellow ochre, viridian, and cadmium yellow.

Identifying Main Centers of Interest

  • Learning to simplify subjects and identify main centers of interest takes practice.
  • I suggest squinting at the subject and gradually opening your eyes to notice which edges and details pop first.
  • My ability to finish the painting quickly comes from having painted similar subjects many times and knowing what to look for.
