Japan Asks America For PERMISSION To Save Its Currency As The Strong Dollar Wrecks Everything

Copy Code

A new currency crisis is emerging globally, exacerbated by the strengthening of the U.S. dollar. This situation is causing a devaluation of other currencies since global transactions often benchmark against the dollar. Since the beginning of the year, the dollar's strength has significantly increased, moving from 100 points to over 106 in just four months. The U.S. benefits from a stronger dollar as it makes imports cheaper, somewhat cushioning the impact of inflation domestically.

Impact on Specific Countries

Japan and South Korea's Economic Challenges

Countries like Japan and South Korea are experiencing panic due to the global devaluation of currencies, prompting them to seek help from the U.S. Their meetings with U.S. Treasury Secretary Yellen were officially about economic ties and countering China, but the real agenda was to seek permission for currency intervention. The Japanese Yen and the South Korean Won have depreciated significantly against the dollar since the start of the year. A depreciating currency benefits exporters by making their goods cheaper on the global market, as evidenced by Japan's export growth. However, a weaker currency also harms the domestic economy by making imports, especially essential goods like food and fuel, more expensive. The continuous devaluation leads to reduced household spending and real wages not keeping up, potentially collapsing the local consumption base. Countries heavily reliant on exports risk becoming overly dependent on U.S. consumers, hollowing out their domestic economies.

Emerging Economies and the Dollar Dilemma

The stronger dollar has caused major Asian currencies to fall, with significant impacts on the cost of importing goods. Countries heavily reliant on exports face the risk of becoming overly dependent on U.S. consumers, which could hollow out their domestic economies.

The Role of Federal Reserve Policies

The main driver behind currency devaluation is the Federal Reserve's interest rate hikes, which have led to significant depreciation of currencies like the Yen and the Won. Rising import costs due to a weaker currency can harm industries by increasing the cost of raw materials, affecting both GDP and local consumption. Persistent inflation in the U.S. means the Federal Reserve is unlikely to cut rates, maintaining the pressure on global currencies. Inflation measures like the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) indicate that inflation remains a significant issue in the U.S., influencing Federal Reserve policies.

The Dilemma of Currency Intervention

Countries like Japan and Indonesia are approaching currency intervention as a solution, which involves dumping US dollars in the markets to prop up their local currencies. However, currency intervention is a temporary measure; as long as the Federal Reserve keeps rates high, local currencies will continue to depreciate. The increase in interest rates has led to a drop in US bond values, causing countries holding US bonds to see their holdings fall by 10-30% in value. If countries like Japan sell their bonds to raise cash, they will realize a significant loss, which is the price of currency intervention. Emerging economies face a dilemma: dump their dollar assets to raise cash and incur a loss, or suffer from a stronger dollar.

Broader Implications and Future Outlook

De-dollarization Efforts

Many economies are trying to de-dollarize bilateral trade to escape the dollar system's negative effects. The shift towards local currencies in international trade, exemplified by Saudi Arabia and China, indicates a move away from the dollar. The yuan is becoming an alternative currency for imports, backed by China's industrial power and export base, which benefits China if the US dollar continues to strengthen.

The Recurring Doom Loop and Alternatives

The global currency crisis is fueled by a U.S. dollar shortage, with the reserve currency status of the dollar creating a recurring doom loop. Countries are moving to trade using local currencies, indicating a shift away from the dollar. Japan and South Korea's attempts at currency intervention and maintaining their currencies' value against the dollar may only provide temporary relief.

* * *

The global currency crisis, driven by the strengthening U.S. dollar, presents a complex challenge for the global economy. Countries like Japan, South Korea, and emerging economies face significant economic pressures, while the role of Federal Reserve policies exacerbates the situation. Currency intervention offers a temporary solution, but the broader implications and future outlook suggest a shift towards de-dollarization and alternative currencies. The ongoing challenges and the uncertain future of global economies in the face of a strengthening U.S. dollar underscore the complexity of navigating the global currency crisis.
