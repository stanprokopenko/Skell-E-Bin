Proportional Differences

Let's start with proportional differences. A blob doesn't dictate a single type of box. For instance, a long blob might suggest a long box, but it could also represent a flat box, like a sketchbook rather than a shoebox. This flexibility allows for various interpretations of the same blob, demonstrating that every step reveals more about the potential box. It's crucial to understand that the blob can evolve into different box types, emphasizing the importance of seeing beyond the initial shape.

Convergence and Foreshortening

Next, let's talk about convergence and foreshortening. These concepts are key to creating depth and perspective in your drawings. By starting with a blob that already suggests depth—like one that's skinny at one end and gets bigger—you can more easily envision a box with foreshortening. This approach helps in imagining how the planes of the box converge and how to represent a box that's either coming towards the viewer or extending away. Remember, the initial blob shape can guide the degree of foreshortening and the direction of the box's planes.

Overlap and Diminution

When introducing multiple boxes into a scene, overlap and diminution become critical for creating depth. By arranging blobs so that some are behind others and varying their sizes, you can imply spatial relationships even before defining the boxes. This technique not only helps in establishing depth but also in creating a sense of gesture and flow within the composition. As you convert these blobs into boxes, consider how their orientation and size contribute to the overall dynamic of the scene.

Maintaining Blob Integrity

An important aspect of this exercise is maintaining the integrity of the original blob shape. The goal is to convert the blob into a box that closely matches the initial shape unless you intentionally decide to modify it. Straying too far from the original blob can detract from the exercise's purpose, which is to practice converting abstract shapes into more defined forms. Therefore, aim to keep your boxes true to the blobs they originated from.

Final Advice

As you practice these concepts, try to stay as close as possible to the original blob shapes and use them as a foundation for your boxes. This approach will help you refine your ability to see and represent three-dimensional forms on a two-dimensional surface. Remember, the more you practice, the easier it becomes to visualize and draw boxes from blobs. And don't worry, I'll be here to provide further guidance and critiques to support your progress.

See you later, and happy drawing!
