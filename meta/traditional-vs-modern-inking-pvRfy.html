Traditional vs Modern Inking

Copy Code

Traditional tools have been used by inkers for many years. These tools include the Series 7 Winsor & Newton sable brush and a nib holder with a Japanese nib, equivalent to the American Hunt mapping point number 102. These tools are known for their ability to provide incredible detail and decent coverage.

Techniques and Tips for Using Traditional Tools

Using traditional tools requires a certain level of skill and control. Here are some tips:

  • Brushes: They allow for incredible detail and decent coverage. Light pressure with brushes avoids thick lines. Adjusting pressure can create textured lines. Brushes can fill in shadow shapes more easily than quills.
  • Nibs: Effective for inking finished pencils but can be messy and difficult to control. Avoid dipping nibs too far into the ink to prevent spills. Crow quills require careful dipping and test strokes. They can scratch the paper and pick up fibers if overused.
  • Control and Patience: Traditional tools provide crisp lines but require significant control. They lay down a lot of ink, requiring drying time to avoid smudges.

Transition to Modern Inking Tools

Modern tools are more portable and less messy compared to traditional tools. Examples include Micron pens and Tombow brush pens.

  • Micron Pens: Allow for quick, consistent lines without worrying about pressure. They can be used like pencils, providing ease and speed. Micron pens can build up line thickness easily and offer some line weight variation with light pressure.
  • Tombow Brush Pens: Provide more control and are easier to use than traditional brushes. The hard tip is more like penciling but gives a thicker line more easily. They don't need to be dipped, providing more control over the line.

Practical Inking Tips and Techniques

Inking requires patience and cleaner lines than penciling. Here are some practical tips:

  • Faithfulness to Original Shapes: Inking over someone else's pencils requires faithfulness to the original shapes. Adding extra detail is tempting but should be avoided to maintain the original artwork.
  • Combining Tools: Using a brush and quill together can provide the benefits of both tools. Flexible rulers and French curves can help with inking precise lines. Beveled edges on rulers prevent ink from bleeding under them.
  • Erasing and Touch-ups: Erasing pencils after inking may require touch-ups. Printing the page for reference can help avoid losing pencil details.

Combining Tools for Effective Inking

Combining different tools can enhance the inking process:

  • Quill and Brush: Using a quill for small details and a brush for larger areas is effective. Switching tools quickly when more effective is important.
  • Dark Areas: For dark areas, use a brush and sometimes mark areas with an X to avoid getting lost.
  • Synthetic Tip Brush: More convenient and less messy than traditional brushes. Squeezing the chamber refills the brush with ink, but be careful not to squeeze too hard.
  • Micron Pens and Tombow Brush Pens: Combining these tools can provide different effects and improve the picture beyond the pencil stage.

Final Touches and Corrections

Final touches and corrections are crucial for a polished piece:

  • Whiteout: Use whiteout for small corrections and to add light effects. A Unipasca paint pen can be used for easy corrections.
  • Panel Borders: Drawing panel borders with a Copic Multiliner is easier than using a quill or brush. Ruling panel borders with a pen is more effective.
  • Detailing: Using a thinner pen for finer details and to clean up harsh contrasts. Adding line weights and shadows to create depth and believability. Using a smaller pen for background rendering adds dimension without looking flat.
  • Erasing Pencils: Erasing pencils reveals how well the inks hold together. Start erasing with a kneaded eraser to avoid fading the inks.
  • Line Weights: Establishing line weights directly in ink saves time. Throwing lines quickly with ink is more enjoyable and gives a nicer line.

Using these techniques and tools allows for a more organic and gestural style, which can be faster and more enjoyable than traditional penciling.
