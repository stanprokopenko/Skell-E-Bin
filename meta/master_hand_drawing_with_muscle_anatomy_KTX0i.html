Muscles of the Hand

Thenar Eminence

The thenar eminence, or the thumb mass on the palm side, is our starting point. It's the most prominent muscle mass in the palm, originating from the wrist and attaching along the thumb bones. This mass is made up of three muscles: Opponens Pollicis, Flexor Pollicis Brevis, and Abductor Pollicis Brevis. Each plays a role in the thumb's movement, with the abductor pollicis brevis being particularly interesting for its ability to abduct and medially rotate the thumb. Understanding these muscles helps us grasp the thumb's complex motions, including flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, opposition, and reposition.

Adjacent to the thenar eminence, the adductor pollicis muscle is worth noting. Though not easily visible, it influences the design of skin folds between the thumb and index finger by adducting the thumb.

Hypothenar Eminence

The hypothenar eminence, or the pinky muscle mass, is narrower and not as thick as the thumb mass. It consists of four muscles: Opponens Digiti Minimi, Flexor Digiti Minimi Brevis, Abductor Digiti Minimi, and Palmaris Brevis. The abductor digiti minimi is the most visible, playing a key role in abducting the pinky. The palmaris brevis, unique for its perpendicular orientation, pulls the skin inward to improve grip.

First Dorsal Interosseous

The first dorsal interosseous muscle fills the gap between the thumb and index finger metacarpals, creating a visible bulge. It abducts the index finger and adds depth to the hand's anatomy. When drawing this area, focus on the overlaps to convey depth accurately.

Additional Muscles

Briefly, the lumbricals are four worm-like muscles on the palm side, originating on the tendons of the flexor digitorum profundus and inserting on the extensor tendons. They're interesting for their role in flexing the metacarpophalangeal joints and extending the interphalangeal joints, though they're rarely visible due to palmar fat.
