Initial Sketching Approach

I started by choosing a classic car from the 1920s or 1930s to draw. My focus was on capturing a three-quarter view angle, paying close attention to details like fenders, wheels, and the windshield. This initial sketch was crucial for understanding a potential angle for drawing vehicles.

Manipulation for Creative Exploration

After laying down the initial sketch, I began manipulating the car's shape and details. This included twisting the front grill and stretching the windshield. My goal was to make the drawing visually more interesting by capturing action, movement, and energy. This process of manipulation allowed me to creatively explore beyond the constraints of reality.

Balancing Observation and Free Drawing

It's important to balance drawing from observation with drawing freely for fun. Both approaches are mixed in my practice. Drawing from observation helps to build a visual vocabulary, while free drawing allows for creative exploration.

Understanding Basic Structures

Drawing familiar subjects, like vehicles or animals, makes it easier to manipulate details and proportions. Understanding the basic structure of these subjects helps in drawing them from various angles without needing to remember every detail. This knowledge facilitates easier manipulation.

Focusing on Reality or Stylization

When it comes to shadow design, the choice between focusing on reality or stylization is intentional. My aim is for consistency in the artwork's aesthetic, whether that leans more towards a realistic portrayal or a stylized one.

Speed and Confidence in Drawing

Speed in drawing comes from familiarity with the subject. This allows for quick, confident strokes without overthinking details. Transitioning from pencil to brush pen was influenced by a class that emphasized building confidence in line work through repetition and familiarity.

Influence of Other Artists

Meeting and working with artists like Kim Jung Gi and others in the Superani collective has influenced my approach and style. This influence comes through shared techniques, interests, and communal learning.

Expressing Motion, Emotion, and Personal Experience

Drawing is not just about logical representation. It's also about expressing motion, emotion, and personal experience. Each sketch is a valuable part of the learning and creative process, allowing for personal expression beyond the constraints of reality.
