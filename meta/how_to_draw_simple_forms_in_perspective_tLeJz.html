How to Draw Simple Forms in Perspective

Copy Code

As a beginner artist, it can be challenging to imagine and draw cubes or other forms in perspective without any reference. Figuring out the angles and how they work together can be frustrating, especially when you start with one line at a time and end up with a result that doesn't look quite right. In this tutorial, I'll share an easier approach to drawing simple forms in perspective that will help you develop your ability to visualize 3D forms in your head.

Starting with a Simple Shape

Instead of trying to figure out the perspective of a box right away, start with a simple shape or "blob" that establishes the size, angle, and tilt of your form. This is much easier to adjust and gives you a foundation to build upon. For example, when drawing a character's torso, start with a rough shape that indicates the general size and tilt of the ribcage. Don't worry about being precise at this stage; just focus on getting the basic shape down.

Indicating Planes

Once you have your basic shape, the next step is to indicate a plane, such as the top plane, to show the viewing angle. This helps determine whether you're looking up at the form, down at it, or straight on. Simply add a simple oval or shape within your basic shape to represent the plane. Again, don't worry about perfect angles or lines at this point.

Showing the Twist

To determine the amount of twist or rotation in your form, add a line that represents the division between the front and side planes. This line should be placed somewhere along the long axis of your basic shape and will create a corner where it intersects with the plane you indicated earlier. The position of this line will show how much the form is twisted or rotated.

Imagining and Refining the Box

With your basic shape, indicated plane, and twist line in place, you can now start to imagine and draw the box based on the established perspective. Use the angles and lines you've created to guide you as you refine the form. Check for proper convergence and proportions, making sure that lines are parallel or converging towards the same point as needed. Feel free to adjust and simplify the form as you go, especially when dealing with organic shapes like ribcages or heads.

Practicing the Technique

To develop your ability to visualize 3D forms, practice this technique repeatedly with various sizes, angles, and twists. Start rough and gradually refine the form, focusing on creating a general framework rather than perfect angles. As you practice, you'll begin to internalize the different angles and perspectives, making it easier to imagine and draw these forms without relying on the step-by-step process.

Remember, the goal is to develop your visual memory and understanding of 3D forms, so don't get discouraged if your first attempts don't look perfect. With practice and patience, you'll soon be able to imagine and draw simple forms in perspective with ease.
