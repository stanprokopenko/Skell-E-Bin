Thinking on Characters

When thinking about environments in your Marvel comic ideas, I like to introduce this concept that I call thinking on the characters. Whether it's Scarlet Witch, Doctor Strange, or any character in the Marvel universe, I try to think about what defines them as characters and how we can tell their story visually without saying a word of dialogue. This approach helps in designing and fleshing out the spaces where the characters exist.

For example, imagine Captain America taking a moment to work on one of his old World War II motorcycles. This scene grounds Captain America in a real way, contrasting with him flexing while punching Red Skull. You might think this seems boring, but trust me, this visual contrast is important for telling a good story. A quiet moment drawn well makes the action scenes more impactful.

Designing Character-Reflective Environments

Immediately, I think about treating the environment like it's its own character. It's not just a background; it's a place where the character literally walks around. What defines Captain America that we can add visually? Start by brainstorming ideas: high-waisted pants, neat, shield, old school, suspenders, patriotic, America. Adding these details breathes life into a character that doesn't actually exist.

Thumbnailing and Choosing the Shot

We're going to think on the characters, choose our shot, and make a map. When I say choose your shot, I mean designing a thumbnail that fits the storytelling moment. For Captain America reflecting on who he is, it's a quiet moment. What kind of shot fits this best? This is why we talked about different perspective grids earlier. We want a foundation to match the scene's vibe with the best environment and background shot.

I read a script and sketch an idea. Maybe a two-point perspective, going wide, with the garage opening dark. As I do this, I think about adding elements that define Captain America. For example, an American flag, Captain America with his hands in his pockets, and a cast shadow. If something works small, it will work big. Keep it simple.

Sometimes, the initial thumbnail might not work. For a quiet scene, a one-point perspective might be better. I might want a top-down shot, with Captain America in shadow, reflecting on his identity. Thumbnailing allows us to explore different ideas quickly.

Creating a Map of the Environment

After thumbnailing, I like to make a map. This step ensures I know where everything is, making it easier if the camera moves or if a fight happens in the scene. For example, in Captain America's garage, I do a quick top-down illustration based on the thumbnail.

Here's a simple map: - Big American flag - Tool bench - Old fridge - Motorcycle - Another tool bench - Something under a tarp

This map helps me understand the space better. If Scarlet Witch is in the scene, I know where everything is, making the drawing process smoother.

Adding Specific Details and Using Reference Material

Next, we scope the small details. This involves finding reference material to get things right. Being intentional with reference makes everything look better and more fun. For example, finding an image of a 1940s fridge helps sell the environment as Captain America's garage.

Don't let this overwhelm you. Find one image that helps and get going. You're not trying to draw a photo; you need specifics to add to your drawing. Artists like Chris Somni excel at this, making simple yet specific drawings that fit the time period.

The Importance of Intentionality in Environment Design

Making environments memorable and impactful involves intentional details. This approach makes environment drawing more fun and adds confidence. We are the budget for everything a reader sees in a comic book scene. Adding small, intentional details brings the story to life.

For instance, a 1950s garage barely changed over time. In movies, this would require an entire set piece. As artists, we need Google and a willingness to draw. Small intentional details on a desk or in a scene make a huge impact, helping readers connect with the story.

By thinking on characters, choosing the right shot, making a map, and adding specific details, we create environments that reflect our characters and enhance our storytelling.
