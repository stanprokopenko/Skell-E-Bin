Drawing an Average Loomis Head from the Front

  • Start with a sphere for the cranial mass.
  • Chop off the sides of the sphere to create a flat side plane.
  • Add the brow line across the middle of the oval.
    • Draw a parallel line below for the nose.
    • Drop another parallel line down for the chin.
  • Attach the jaw from the brow to the chin.
  • Divide the jaw into front and side planes.

There you go. There's a nice Loomis head. This is a nice guide to then help me put features on top of that.

Adapting the Loomis Method for a Head Looking Down

  • Begin with the cranial ball.
  • Adjust the side plane chop based on the side-to-side turn of the head.
  • Tilt and narrow the oval to match the head angle.
  • Wrap the brow line around the head following the tilt.
  • Add parallel lines for the nose and chin, accounting for foreshortening.
    • The thirds will get smaller as they go away from you, but don't push it too much unless the camera is very close.
  • Define the hairline by curving a parallel line around the top of the head.

Drawing a Perfect Side View Loomis Head

  • Start with the cranial ball.
  • Place the side plane oval in the middle of the circle.
    • The side plane is about two-thirds of the height of the full circle.
  • Drop a line from forehead to chin to define the jaw angle.
  • Add parallel lines for the brow, nose, and chin.
  • Attach the jaw, ensuring the bottom aligns with the neck.
  • Note that the height and depth of the head are equal from the nose tip to the back of the skull.

I like to bring out the muzzle of the lips and teeth, then back in for the chin. From a side view, the eye socket is just a rectangular shape. The top of the cheekbone is like the bottom of the eye socket. I like this rhythm and it goes to the top of the ear.
