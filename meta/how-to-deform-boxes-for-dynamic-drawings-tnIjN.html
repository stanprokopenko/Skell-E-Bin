How to Deform Boxes for Dynamic Drawings

Copy Code

Perspective doesn't have to be static and boring. We can add gesture to our perspective and deform our boxes to make them feel more dynamic. I see two main use cases for this technique:

  1. Drawing organic subjects like the human body
  2. Adding personality to inorganic objects like houses, cars, or toys

By deforming boxes, we can bring these forms to life and add a sense of movement or character. Let's explore six methods to make our perspective drawings more dynamic.

Curving

Curving involves adding a C-curve to a box. Instead of drawing straight lines, we curve them to create a more fluid shape. Here's how to do it:

  1. Start with the front plane of a box
  2. Instead of drawing straight lines for the sides, curve them outward
  3. Connect the corners with curved lines

Think of it as two boxes at different angles, with everything in between curving to connect the corners. This technique works great for arms, snakes, or any animal torso that needs a slight curve.

You can apply subtle curves to typically straight objects too. For example, a tall office building could have a very slight curve to give it personality in a cartoon world.

Bloating

Bloating is like inflating a box with air. All sides curve outward, creating a rounded, expanded appearance. To bloat a box:

  1. Start with a basic box shape
  2. Curve all edges outward
  3. Add cross contours that wrap around the inflated form

Bloated forms feel safe, cute, and friendly - perfect for designing cute characters.

Imploding

Imploding is the opposite of bloating. We're sucking air out of the box, curving all sides inward. This creates sharp, rigid forms that can feel dangerous or villainous. To implode a box:

  1. Start with a basic box
  2. Curve all edges inward
  3. Curve inner planes inward as well

Tapering

Tapering involves making one side of a form thicker or thinner. This can add weight to the bottom of an object or create imbalance. To taper a box:

  1. Draw the basic box shape
  2. Make one side wider or narrower
  3. Adjust the connecting lines accordingly

Tapering is useful for body parts like forearms (thicker at top, thinner at wrist) or calves.

Twisting

Twisting is particularly useful for drawing torsos. We rotate the top and bottom planes in different directions. Here's how:

  1. Draw the top plane (e.g., shoulders)
  2. Draw the bottom plane (e.g., hips) rotated in a different direction
  3. Connect the edges, making them curve to accommodate the twist

Combine twisting with other techniques like curving or bloating to create more complex organic forms.

Leaning

Leaning isn't a deformation, but it adds gesture to combinations of boxes. Simply tilt boxes at different angles to create a sense of movement or personality. You can stack leaning boxes to create curved gestures with straight-edged forms.

Applying These Techniques

Let's put these methods into practice by drawing a dog house:

  1. Start with a basic house shape, tapering the front to make it top-heavy
  2. Curve some edges to add personality
  3. Deform the roof, making it peek up at the front and flatten at the back
  4. Add details like a door and name board

Remember, we're not randomly deforming things. We're still following perspective rules, but breaking them slightly to enhance the visual effect. It takes practice to find the right balance between realism and dynamic deformation.

By mastering these techniques, you can bring your perspective drawings to life, adding character and movement to both organic and inorganic subjects. Experiment with different combinations to develop your own unique style. Have fun playing with shapes and forms - suddenly, boxes can be exciting again!
