How to Draw Portraits Rapidly with Mass Drawing

Hi, I'm Stephen Bauman, and in this video, I'm going to show you a technique called mass drawing. It's one of the fastest ways to get a portrait drawing on your paper and recognizable. It's also the newest addition to my block-in course on proko.com. In this demo, I'll work fast and rough, sharing insights and techniques that make mass drawing so effective for portraiture.

Here's the thing about mass drawing: it's incredibly rapid. It's a lot like painting. Think of your pencil as a paintbrush the size of your index finger.

Initial Steps in Mass Drawing

I start with a series of value shapes that roughly represent the shadow below the nose, the shadow of the eye socket on the left-hand side, and maybe up the edge of the temporal line. Even at this point, we can start taking measurements. For instance, I want to know where the back edge of the cast shadow lines up with the shadow shape in the upper inner corner of the eye socket. I use the edge of my pencil to measure this.

You can choose to do this now or later, but I take my stump and punch up the core shadow a bit. This serves as an anchor point. In block-ins and early drawings, you want a point of truth. Since we're not working from the outside in, our point of truth happens inside the drawing. I use the eye socket as a point of truth, a unit of measurement.

Developing the Drawing

With the eye socket as my point of truth, I measure the length from the top of the brow ridge down to the shadow on the nose. I adjust the distance accordingly. Next, I measure from the cheekbone down to the outside corner of the mouth, and maybe all the way down to the chin. I compare these measurements to ensure accuracy.

I block in all the value that comes after this, using the side of my pencil. Avoid using lines; focus on the meetings of values. This might be difficult if you're not used to drawing this way, but in mass drawing, you're dealing with values, not lines.

Refining the Drawing

I incorporate a value in the background to give a full view of the light shape of the head. This stage involves a reasonable expectation of inaccuracy. I know my block-in isn't perfect and will need changes. Mass drawing relies on a strong sense of naive recognition. If your picture resembles your source image a bit, you can make adjustments based on likeness without taking measurements.

Detailed Measurements and Adjustments

Now, we need to make a bunch of measurements. I use a harder pencil (an F lead) to triangulate. Every shape exists in reference to a non-objective grid, lining up horizontally and vertically. For example, the apex of the light shape of the cheekbone lines up with something along the contour. I break down and triangulate these distances to make the abstract phase as accurate as possible.

Finalizing the Drawing

I mass in the eyes, leaving the eyelid out for now. I push the value out on the left-hand side to eventually make a shape around the ear, anchoring the head in space. Using a saturated stump, I remove light value from darker areas. All triangulation should happen now because we won't want to change things later.

I use a mono zero eraser to carve in where the eyelids will be. For the ear, I measure from the outside corner of the nostril to the high point of the ear, comparing it to the light shape of the forehead. I ensure the ear's value is correct, replacing any erased value.

Practical Tips for Mass Drawing

  • Good Light and Shadow Dichotomy: Your source image or model setup should have a strong contrast of shadow and light. This helps divide the image into large flat shapes of dark and light.
  • Recommended Materials: Start with a 4B or 6B pencil for strong contrast. These soft grades of graphite are easy to manipulate with a paper stump.
  • Practice: The first attempt might not be successful. Aim for at least five or six reps to understand how mass drawing fits into your workflow.

Integration with Other Block-In Techniques

Mass drawing is one of four block-in styles you'll practice in the block-in course. The other styles are visual, structural, and hybrid. Practice and understand each style separately to integrate them into your drawing process and become a proficient portrait artist.
