Drawing 3D Forms from Simple Shapes

In this episode, I'm diving into a method that makes drawing simple forms in perspective more approachable for beginner artists. It's common to struggle with imagining cubes or any form in perspective from scratch. You might start drawing, only to find that the angles don't come together as you intended. This can be frustrating, but I've got a technique that will help you develop your ability to visualize these 3D forms in your head, making the drawing process much smoother.

Understanding Perspective and Simple Forms

Important: Before diving into complex angles and perspectives, it's crucial to start with imagining 3D forms and developing the ability to visualize them in your head. This approach simplifies the process, making it less intimidating for beginners.

Drawing a Character's Torso

When drawing a character, instead of starting with precise angles, begin with a rough shape to establish the size and angle of the torso. This method allows you to adjust easily and provides a clear direction for the form's orientation.

  • Indicate a Plane: After establishing the rough shape, the next step is to indicate a plane. For example, if you're looking down at a character, draw a simple oval to represent the top plane of the torso. This doesn't require perfect angles or a detailed box yet.

  • Determine the Twist: To understand the form's orientation fully, you need to indicate the twist. This can be done by drawing a line that divides the front plane from the side plane, giving you a clearer idea of the form's 3D structure.

  • Refine the Form: With the basic orientation established, you can start refining the form into a more defined shape. This doesn't require drawing a perfect box but rather a general shape that hints at the form's perspective.

Applying the Technique to Other Forms

This approach isn't limited to torsos; it can be applied to other forms like the pelvis. Start with a blobby shape to indicate the general size and position, then adjust the proportions as needed. Indicating top and side planes helps establish the perspective without needing to draw perfect angles.

Finalizing and Cleaning Up

Once you have the rough form down, you can go over it with clean lines, fixing any errors and ensuring the angles converge properly. This step allows you to refine the drawing while still keeping the perspective accurate.

Simplifying Complex Forms

Even complex forms like the head can be simplified using this technique. Start with a general shape, add a cap to indicate the top plane, and then define the front and side planes. This simplifies the process, making it easier to visualize the form in perspective.

Practice and Application

To develop your ability to visualize 3D forms, practice drawing multiple forms with varying sizes, angles, and twists. This exercise will help you slowly improve your visualization skills, making it easier to draw forms in perspective.

* * *

This approach offers a more accessible way for beginners to start drawing in perspective. By starting rough and gradually refining the form, you can avoid the intimidation of perfect angles and develop your visualization skills. Give it a try and see how it improves your drawing process.
