4 Steps to Draw the Head from Any Angle

Welcome, I'm Michael Hampton. Today we're going to be talking about head drawing and construction. Specifically, we're going to talk about the basic setup. I go through about four steps to initiate the basic lay-in or gesture of a head drawing. This will cover just about every position that I could potentially see or set a head in. I've included a couple of skulls as reference points to help simplify these big ideas into manageable pieces.

Step 1: Cranial Mass

The first step is always dealing with the cranial mass, which is about two-thirds of the head. The easiest way to manage this is by starting with a basic sphere.

  • Pros of using a sphere: Easier to draw.
  • Cons: Not very exact in terms of representing the human skull.

A more accurate representation would be an egg shape. From a bird's eye view, the egg shape is more tapered and has a flat forehead, with the widest part towards the back of the skull. This shape is more reflective of the cranial mass.

  • Natural tilt: The skull naturally tilts and isn't perfectly horizontal or vertical.
  • Gender differences: Men tend to have a more diagonal forehead tilt, while women have a more vertical tilt.

You can also customize the cranial mass for character archetypes. For example, a square head might suit a stubborn character, while a longer, thinner rectangle might feel more elegant.

Step 2: Tilts

The second step is considering tilts. Early on, I often drew heads that were completely vertical, which looked static and lifeless. Tilts add expression and dynamism to the head drawing.

  • Tilts: Think of the side-to-side lean of the head, neck, and shoulders.
  • Expression: Tilts can convey a lot of non-verbal emotion.
  • Historical context: In 17th-century portraiture, tilts were used to indicate arrogance. Today, we see similar tilts in selfies.

For example, once I have the cranial mass, I add a 2D axis to indicate the lean. This helps keep the head drawing dynamic and expressive.

Step 3: Jaw and T/Cross

The third step is introducing the jaw and using a T or cross for symmetry and proportion.

  • Front view: Pull the jaw down with C curves off the initial sphere, terminating at a straight line for the chin.
  • Three-quarter view: The straight line of the chin shifts towards one side.
  • Profile view: The chin becomes a point, indicating the turn of the jaw.
  • Back three-quarter view: The jaw comes in front of the skull, showing the back or underside of the mandible.

The T or cross helps find the center line of the chin and the symmetry line of the face, splitting it in half. The horizontal line aligns with the eyebrows.

Drawing from Below

  1. Cranial mass: Start with a sphere.
  2. Tilt: Indicate the head leaning back.
  3. Jaw: Adjust the jaw shape, moving the chin up into the skull.
  4. Ellipse: Use an ellipse to indicate perspective, inspired by Andrew Loomis. The ellipse should go from the brow to the top of the ear.

Drawing from Above

  1. Cranial mass: Start with a sphere.
  2. Tilt: Indicate the head leaning forward.
  3. Jaw: Adjust the jaw shape, making the neck longer and more S-like.
  4. Ellipse: Use an ellipse to indicate perspective, with the ear higher than the brow.

Common Mistakes

  • Major axis: Ensure the major axis of the ellipse is at the center of the sphere. This helps maintain proportion and avoid a warped look.

* * *

To recap, the four steps are:

  1. Cranial Mass: Start with a sphere or egg shape.
  2. Tilts: Add expression through head, neck, and shoulder tilts.
  3. Jaw and T/Cross: Introduce the jaw and use a T or cross for symmetry and proportion.
  4. Perspective: Consider perspective with ellipses for above or below views.

This approach is flexible and adaptable. Practice these steps to refine your technique and make your head drawings more dynamic and expressive.
