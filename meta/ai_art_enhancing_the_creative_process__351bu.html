Ethical and Legal Considerations

Ethical concerns about AI and art often stem from issues with excessive capitalism, suggesting that competition is only ethical if it doesn't involve profit from copied work. There's a distinction between individuals copying work and corporations profiting from such actions, with the latter being more problematic. Art lacks a structured definition, leading to debates about what constitutes art and the need for creative legislation to address new technological developments.

Arguments against AI image generation include claims of stealing art, putting artists out of jobs, and the notion that AI cannot create "true" art. Legally, theft involves depriving someone of scarce resources, which doesn't apply to digital art since copying doesn't remove the original work from its owner. AI image generators are argued not to steal or plagiarize since they generate new images by learning from a dataset, not by directly copying or blending existing artworks. The process of AI image generation is likened to human learning and creativity, where both humans and AI learn from existing works to create new ones.

Critics of AI in art often conflate different uses of AI, failing to distinguish between generating new images and directly copying existing ones. The debate includes whether AI's method of creating art constitutes plagiarism or if it's a new form of creativity that parallels human artistic development. Concerns about AI replacing human artists focus on the loss of the human process and effort in art creation, rather than the mere outcome of that process.

The argument shifts between AI being accused of plagiarizing and the fear that AI undermines the value of human artistic effort and creativity. The discussion touches on the philosophical and practical differences between how AI and humans learn and create, with AI being static in its learning once trained. The ethical debate around AI and art is complex, involving legal, philosophical, and practical considerations about creativity, ownership, and the future of art.

Concerns over consent and the use of artists' work in training AI question the ethical and legal aspects of using creators' data without explicit permission. The argument that posting art on public platforms implies consent for it to be seen and used on the internet is presented. It's argued that it's not inherently immoral to train AI on data acquired without consent, as artists implicitly consent to their work being used by posting it online.

Philosophical Debates

What constitutes art and the role of AI in creative processes are central to the philosophical debates surrounding AI and art. The lack of a structured definition of art leads to discussions on the need for creative legislation to adapt to technological advancements. The debate includes whether AI's method of creating art constitutes plagiarism or represents a new form of creativity that parallels human artistic development.

The nature of creativity and learning in humans versus AI is explored through the comparison of AI image generation to human learning and creativity. Both humans and AI learn from existing works to create new ones, but AI is static in its learning once trained. This raises questions about the philosophical and practical differences between how AI and humans learn and create.

Materialism, spiritualism, and objectivism as theories of art are critiqued for their limitations in addressing AI art. Objectivism is introduced as a theory that values art's role in human life, suggesting a more inclusive view of AI-generated art.

The role of art in concretizing metaphysical views is discussed, with art serving as a bridge between complex concepts and sensory perception. AI can generate images that concretize specific metaphysical views, demonstrating the potential for technology to embody complex philosophical concepts in visual form.

Aesthetic judgment and the criteria for good art involve selectivity, clarity, and integration. Good art is distinguished not just by its ability to embody a philosophy but by its aesthetic value, which is independent of the philosophy it conveys. AI-generated art, particularly when using simple text prompts, may lack the selectivity and integration necessary for it to be considered good art. Advanced AI features, like image-to-image generation, offer more control and specificity, potentially leading to high-quality AI-generated art.

Practical Impacts

AI's impact on artists' livelihoods and job security is a significant concern, with fears that AI image generators could replace human artists, leading consumers to prefer AI-generated art over human-created art. This fear is criticized as morally flawed, as opposing technological advancement to preserve jobs opposes economic efficiency and human progress. The resistance to AI in art is likened to protectionism in trade, where inefficiency is protected at the cost of broader societal benefits.

The potential for AI to assist rather than replace human artists is acknowledged, with examples of AI being used creatively in projects. AI technology has been utilized in movies to redub them in multiple languages while maintaining the original voice performances and movements, making art more globally accessible. Veteran Disney animator Aaron Blaze contemplates using AI for adding shadows and highlights to artwork, potentially saving artists time for other creative tasks.

The debate over effort and skill in AI-generated versus traditional art focuses on the effort required to produce art, with some arguing that AI-generated art lacks the effort inherent in traditional art forms. However, the complexity and philosophical depth that can be achieved through human-guided AI art creation challenge this critique. The training and work behind AI models by engineers should also be considered in evaluating AI-generated art.

The role of AI in democratizing art creation and overcoming physical limitations is defended as a positive development, enabling those with physical limitations or lack of time to create art. AI art is seen as a tool for rapid iteration and exploration of creative ideas, enhancing creativity and efficiency. The accessibility of AI art challenges traditional gatekeeping in the art world and allows individuals who struggle with traditional drawing techniques to conceptualize ideas without needing to learn new artistic skills.

Backlash and controversy within the art community over the use of AI includes significant backlash and bullying, with instances of creators being blacklisted for incorporating AI into their work. The controversy extends to YouTubers using AI for tasks like creating thumbnails, criticized as lazy despite the efficiency and practicality AI offers for such applications. The backlash often stems from a misunderstanding of the technology's capabilities and a fear of change within the artistic community.

Societal Implications

The broader debate on innovation versus tradition in art reflects tensions between progress and preservation, challenging the integration of new technologies into established industries. Critics of innovation selectively oppose AI where it threatens specific professions, revealing a double standard and a protectionist attitude.

Ethical concerns about AI and job protectionism involve arguments that ethical AI should not compete with human artists, thereby protecting their jobs. This stance is criticized as morally flawed, opposing economic efficiency and human progress for the sake of preserving jobs.

The impact of AI on the art industry and consumer choice is significant, with AI potentially democratizing art creation and challenging traditional gatekeeping. The critique extends to protectionist policies that benefit inefficient producers at the expense of innovation and consumer choice.

Resistance to AI as a form of protectionism and its historical parallels likens the opposition to AI in art to past resistance against technological advancements, such as the Luddites' opposition to the mechanical loom. This resistance is seen as opposing human flourishing and innovation.

The potential for AI to change the criteria for what is considered "real" art is highlighted by the controversy over AI-generated art raising questions about originality, creativity, and the ethical use of technology in artistic processes. The debate includes whether AI can truly replace human creativity, especially in producing high-quality art, and whether AI-generated images can be considered art based on theories of materialism, spiritualism, and objectivism.
