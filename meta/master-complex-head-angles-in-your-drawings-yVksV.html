Basic Shapes and Initial Steps

Start by looking at the back of the skull. A three-quarter back view is one of the standard views. Begin with a ball, an egg, and then the tilt. Representing the head as an egg helps you notice the natural inclination for the head to sit at a slight diagonal. The front of the face would be at a slight angle, and the attachment for the jaw or TMJ goes in a specific direction. Use mental gymnastics to land on one diagonal that is not stiff.

Understanding the Neck and Shoulders

Tilt the neck in the same direction as the spine. The cervical section of the spine pushes the neck forward, giving it a three-dimensional positioning. Next, add the shoulders. Add the shape for the jaw, front, three-quarter chin, and bring it back up. Use the corner of the chin to aim the line near the ear. Sometimes, draw through to challenge yourself to see it through. Show the base of the skull as a substitute for the cross of the brow. Include the neck to help with overlaps, as overlaps help in getting the big stuff to read.

Techniques for Foreshortening and Dynamic Poses

Emphasize the overlapping of forms like a mini landscape. Surface lines help connect the head to the neck and into the back or rib cage. Using four steps early on limits means but helps in summarizing broad information. The more you practice, the more comfortable you become with this language of drawing. Use perspective arrows to help connect with the idea of forward-back lean to the head. Shoulders and neck positioning are reliable. Use arrows for visual thinking.

Using Perspective Arrows and Lines

Subtract distance for the jaw or face, chin. Pinch in the back, stretch in the front for the neck. Pinch and stretch also take place inside the form. Compressing space for foreshortening is challenging but necessary. Brow to ear indicates a three-dimensional tilt. Set up parameters to build features within the established perspective. Indicate the lean of the shoulders with a plane. The shoulder girdle structure sits over the ribcage. For dramatic looks, keep the tilts simple. Use ellipses to suggest under planes.

Simplifying Complex Forms

Use C for pinch or active muscle, S for stretch or passive muscle. Drawing is about the head's relationship to the negative space. Balance the design of the shape and the space around it. Connect the neck to the rib cage and fill in with anatomy. Use the back plane of the skull to define depth. Maintain parallel lines for depth description. Consider the head as boxes to maintain space and proportion in perspective. Overlapping forms are crucial for foreshortening.

Practical Tips and Exercises

  • Practice regularly to become comfortable with the drawing language.
  • Use abstract shapes for highly foreshortened poses.
  • Build volume with the neck and flare the cylinder for forced perspective.
  • Use the seventh cervical vertebrae as a reference point.
  • Keep the language of drawing simple and clear.
  • Think about the head's relationship to the negative space.
  • Use perspective connections for the neck into the rib cage.
  • For profile views, use the brow or cross to find the back plane of the skull.
  • Maintain parallel lines for depth description.
  • Use C and S curves for the neck.
  • For extreme foreshortening, use overlapping forms.

By following these steps and tips, you'll be able to master drawing the head and neck from challenging perspectives, achieving accurate perspective, foreshortening, and dynamic poses. Keep practicing and refining your skills!
