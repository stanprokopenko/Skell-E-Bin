How to Design an Asgardian

Initial Design Process

When I'm tasked with designing a new Asgardian character, my goal is to stay true to the established theme without making significant alterations. Here's how I approach the initial design process:

  • I start by quickly sketching a rough design, then step away from it. Later, I revisit the sketch to assess my feelings towards it and decide whether to proceed or start over.
  • Envisioning the character's pose is a priority for me. I spread my focus across the core, arms, legs, and head simultaneously.
  • I begin with the eyes and facial features because they help dictate the character's expression and the overall direction of the design.
  • My preference is to design as I draw, rather than sketching thumbnails or complete drawings beforehand. This approach varies depending on time and comfort.
  • For the initial sketch, I use red to differentiate the planning phase from the final details, which I add with a lead pencil.

Structural Considerations

Getting the structure right is crucial. Here's what I focus on:

  • Symmetry and proportion are key. I lay out large shapes first before adding detailed enhancements.
  • Character design requests, which occur about 5-10% of the time, often require quick, on-the-fly adjustments to ensure accuracy and coherence.
  • It's important to sketch the full anatomy to ensure proper structure and alignment before diving into design specifics.
  • Establishing large areas and their correct placement is emphasized before refining details.

Detailing and Refinement

After the basic pose and structure are established, I move on to detailing and refinement:

  • Design details, such as patterns and textures, are considered with inspiration often drawn from research or existing designs.
  • The overarching goal is to create something visually appealing or "cool," using references as needed to spark creativity.
  • When designing armor or accessories, I aim for unique patterns that complement the character's overall aesthetics, focusing on large pieces before adding finer details.
  • The process involves dynamic, on-the-fly adjustments without strict adherence to tools like rulers, aiming for a natural and energetic portrayal.
  • Rotating the drawing helps in accurately capturing tricky perspectives, such as hands.

Inspiration and References

Inspiration and references play a significant role in the design process:

  • I use references to spark creativity and draw inspiration from research or existing designs.
  • Inspirations for patterns and designs can come from admired artists, such as Jim Lee, and the collaborative influence of peers.

Finalizing the Design

Moving towards finalizing the design involves several key steps:

  • After establishing the basic layout, I focus on refining details and considering lighting effects, with a top-down light source being a standard approach.
  • Common design challenges include balancing polished design with solid structural foundations, emphasizing the importance of referencing and anatomical accuracy.
  • Rendering involves considering underlying body structures and how they integrate with the costume, aiming for a cohesive representation.
  • The inking process will refine and clarify the initial sketches, with communication between artist and inker ensuring clarity and fidelity to the vision.

Career Insights and Networking

The art industry is full of fluctuations, and here are some insights:

  • Career fluctuations are normal, with opportunities ranging from major projects to character designs. Visibility and networking are key to attracting work.
  • Participating in Artist Alley at comic conventions can lead to significant opportunities through direct exposure to potential collaborators.

* * *

Creating something new on a blank sheet is a unique and valuable skill. The importance of practice, collaboration, and enjoyment in the art process cannot be overstated.
